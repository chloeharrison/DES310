    6. Enacted and Emergent Narrative

    There are two types of narrative in interactive design:

    Enacted narratives are those where the story itself may be structured around the user’s movement through a site and the features of the environment may react according to their actions. Websites like Amazon, EBay, or basically any shopping or service site are enacted narrative spaces. They are all sites that want the users to move through the site towards a specific set of actions like buying something or making contact with someone.

    Emergent narratives are those where spaces are designed to be rich with narrative potential, allowing users to create their own story or journey. Sites like Facebook, Flickr, Twitter, or Pinterest are emergent narrative spaces. They are all web applications that encourage their users use the tools at their disposal to tell their own story or create their own journey. The users define how they want to use the narrative space, which regularly has unexpected results.

WHAT IS NARRATIVE? LEARNING FROM MUSIC AND FILM

Learning Summary

In this week's lecture we looked learning from music and film. Films and music follow a similar type of narrative technique to any narrative or story. Generally they all begin with a problem, have a middle and find a solution at the end, the only thing that is different is how it is delivered. Films generally follow a few different structure which are:

Three-Act Structure

This is a pretty old method that is used when telling stories and is frequently used within movies, plays, poetry and novels. The general layout begins with a setup, has some form of problem within the middle and ends with a resolution.

Exposition

Exposition is when during the first scene of the movie all of the main characters are introduced to the viewers, during this time the plot is also uncovered.

Climax and Resolution

This is when the final confrontation happens with the last scene, For example, the hero fighting the villain. Whatever happens during this last scene will end the film.

Inverted Pyramid

The Inverted pyramid is a narrative structure that is mainly used within journalism. Like a pyramid it priorities information into a triangle shape. The most important information being at the top which would be the lead of the story. This structure is commonly used for news reports within new papers because it is a useful way to structure out the information.

Music

Just like film, music has a structure. Generally the majority of songs begin with an intro then go into a verse and then a chorus. Musicians, like web designs structure their work, for example they will have an intro, verse and chorus whereas we would have a head, body and footer.

What is the Takahe Bird?

The South Island takahē, notornis, or takahē (Porphyrio hochstetteri), is a flightless bird indigenous to New Zealand and belonging to the rail family. It was thought to be extinct after the last four known specimens were taken in 1898. However, after a carefully planned search effort the bird was rediscovered near Lake Te Anau in the Murchison Mountains, South Island, on 20 November 1948. The adult takahē is mainly purple-blue in color, with a greenish back and inner wings. It has a red frontal shield and red-based pink bill. The legs are pink. Sexes are similar, the females being slightly smaller, but young birds have mainly pale brown plumage. Immature birds have a pinkish bill with a bluish cast. This is a noisy species with a loud clowp call.

The species is still present in the location where it was rediscovered in the Murchison Mountains. Small numbers have also been successfully translocated to five predator-free offshore islands, Tiritiri Matangi, Kapiti, Maud, Mana and Motutapu, where they can be viewed by the public. As of October 2016 there were 306 takahē accounted for, showing slow but steady growth over the previous few years. The takahē is monogamous, with pairs remaining together from 12 years to, probably, their entire lives. It builds a bulky nest under bushes and scrub, and lays one to three buff eggs. The chick survival rate is 73-97%.

The near-extinction of the formerly widespread takahē is due to a number of factors: over-hunting, loss of habitat and introduced predators have all played a part. The introduction of red deer (Cervus elaphus) represent a severe competition for food, while the stoats (Mustela erminea) take a role as predators. Since the species is long-lived, reproduces slowly, takes several years to reach maturity, and had a large range that has drastically contracted in comparatively few generations, inbreeding depression is a significant problem.

Who is Dr Geoffrey Buckland Orbell?

Geoffrey Buckland Orbell MBE was born on 7 October 1908 and died on 15 August 2007 was a New Zealand doctor and keen tramper/bush walker best known for the rediscovery of the takahē in 1948. The takahē was widely thought to be extinct but Orbell suspected it might survive. While taking time off from his Invercargill practice to search for the takahē, he discovered a set of unfamiliar footprints. After following the footprints with three companions he rediscovered the species on 20 November 1948 in a remote valley of the Murchison Mountains near Lake Te Anau.

What is Typography?

Typography is the art and technique of arranging type an also the look of a bit of text. Typography is important to the work and skills of a designer and is about much more than making the words legible. The choice of typeface and how it’s made to work and look with layout, grid, colour scheme, design theme and will make the difference between a good, bad and great design.

White Space

The blank areas on a page where text and illustrations are not printed. White space should be considered an important graphic element in page design.White space which is also know as negative space is the part of a page The term came from graphic design practices when printing process generally used white paper and thus the term “white space” came from the blank parts of the page that were left. It is important that white space isn’t just space that has been left blank for the sake of it. It is an important element within design practice that is used to make aesthetically pleasing compositions. If it is used incorrectly it may look as if the work is incomplete or simply badly designed.

Within adverting white space is expensive. Many companies want to get as much onto their advertisement as possible which may result in a cluttered design, but in order to make it look good it will cost them to go bigger. The likes of newspapers, yellow pages and some books really don’t take white space into consideration because they need to get as much onto one page as possible in order to cut costs. However, this lack of white space results in cramped, hard to read text which can be very frustrating for readers. It is for this reason that brans that have a tendency to be known as most “luxurious” have adverts that have a lot of white space because they want the main focus to be their product or slogan, not a busy page crammed with text.

A fantastic example of white space at its best is the worlds leading search engine, Google. Their homepage is so simple and elegant because it used white space in such an effect way. By only having a text box and their logo, the user will not be distracted by text or images when they o to the site to search for something. Whereas on the other hand we have Yahoo, who seem as if they have never heard of white space. Their site is so busy and crammed full of information that it completely takes you of your focus of what you were going to look for in the first place.

Key Principles of Typography

Typography is what a lot of designers use when creating things. Typography is the art form and technique that entails arranging type with the purpose of making language more visible. There are five main principles of typography that I am going to explain below.

  1. Don’t use too many typefaces

    2. A typeface is a collection of fonts put together for example Arial, while a font might just be one kind of Arial like Arial Black. The difference between these two are huge and can easily be mixed up when talking about typography. The three most popular typefaces nowadays are Serif, Sans Serif and monospace. A serif is a small line attached to the end of a stroke in a letter or a symbol. If the letters and symbols of a typeface have serifs, then we call it a serif typeface. It has been said that the strokes of a serif typeface help to guide the eye when reading. Serifs are a standard when it comes to most newspapers. Sans-Serif however is said to be easier on the eye when reading online.

    Sans Serif typefaces don’t have these thin lines hence the word “Sans” in front of Serif which means “Without” in French. Serif is known for its serious and professional look Sans Serif is known for being modern and vibrant. Because of their simplicity they are used a lot in body text as they are easy to read even in small fonts. Some examples of Sans Serif are Arial, Verdana and Tahoma. Here’s a comparison between the too.

    Monospace which is often used in webpages as it is plainer compared to the other too and it enhances readability. Each character shares the same width hence the name Monospace. Some examples of Monospace fonts are Courier, Consolas and Andale Mono.

  2. Contrast is good, but the wrong colours can make the design not look appealing and harder to read.The most common form that is encounter is text with black over a white background. However many people like to have bright colours sometimes this is not applicable because colour can make text harder to read which makes the experience less enjoyable.

    3. Contrast is a good thing however keeping the background simple is a good choice as it allows for optimal results and means that the text is legible.

  3. Limited use of display faces

    4. Display typefaces are very interesting. However keep in mind that display typefaces were not designed to be used for bodies of text but generally require a larger font to be readable. A good way of remembering is that these faces tend to be more complex, thus tiring the viewer’s eyes easily.

  4. Scannable text is a must

    5. The purpose is to make sure that the text is in such good shape that the reader will keep interest long enough to read through the whole thing because they can click away at any time. The main points that will keep the users attention are things like headers, graphical elements and pieces of emphasised text. The main things that will impact how scannable your text is the focus points like text size and contrast.

  5. Don’t distort typefaces

    6. Sometimes individuals feel the need to make text fit a certain area by stretching the text however this is not always the best thing to do as this takes away from the legibility which takes away from what is being created

Grid Based Layout

A grid subdivides a page vertically and horizontally into margins, columns, inter-column spaces, lines of type, and spaces between blocks of type and images. These subdivisions form the basis of a modular and systematic approach to the layout, particularly for multipage documents, making the design process quicker, and ensuring visual consistency between related pages. structures in which their sensibility can flourish. It is commonly seen in newspaper and magazine layout with columns of text and images. One grid, or a collection of grids, may be used across an entire project to achieve a consistent look and feel. In a finished product, the grid is invisible, but following it helps in creating good print and web layouts. There is no limit to the grid layouts that can be created. Common types include equally sized two, three and four-column grids with a header across the top, as well as a full-page grid of squares. From these building blocks, the variation of column widths, borders, page size and other features of the grid will lead to unique page design.

The Golden Ratio

The Golden Ratio is one where the ratio of the smaller segment to the larger segment is the same as the larger segment to the sum of both segments. This is more easily seen in a simple diagram.

It’s believed that the Golden Ratio has been in use for at least 4,000 years in human art and design, but it may be even longer than that – some people argue that the Ancient Egyptians used the principle to build the pyramids. In more contemporary times, the Golden Ratio can be observed in music, art, and design all around you. By applying a similar working methodology you can bring the same design sensibilities to your own work. Here are just a couple of examples of The Golden Ratio:

The Parthenon

Ancient Greek architecture used the Golden Ratio to determine pleasing dimensional relationships between the width of a building and its height, the size of the portico and even the position of the columns supporting the structure. The final result is a building that feels entirely in proportion. The neo-classical architecure movement reused these principles too.

The Last Supper

Leonardo da Vinci, like many other artists throughout the ages, made extensive use of the Golden Ratio to create pleasing compositions. In the last supper, the figures are arranged in the lower two thirds (the larger of the two parts of the Golden Ratio), and the position of Jesus is perfectly plotted by arranging golden rectangles across the canvas.

Nature

There are also numerous examples of the Golden Ratio in nature – you can observe the ratio all around you. Flowers, sea-shells, pineapples, and even honeycombs all exhibit the same principle ratio in their make-up. So using the Golden Ratio in your design work is both appropriate and foreshadowed in our everyday environment.

UX and UI design

To be honest I have heard of the term UX and UI dsign but never really fully understood what they were and the different between the two so I have taken a look to understand what they mean.

UX Design refers to the term User Experience Design, while UI Design stands for User Interface Design. Both elements are crucial to a product and work closely together. But despite their professional relationship, the roles themselves are quite different, referring to very different parts of the process and the design discipline. Where UX Design is a more analytical and technical field, UI Design is closer to what we refer to as graphic design, though the responsibilities are somewhat more complex.

There is an analogy I like to use in describing the different parts of a (digital) product: If you imagine a product as the human body, the bones represent the code which give it structure. The organs represent the UX design: measuring and optimizing against input for supporting life functions. And UI design represents the cosmetics of the body–its presentation, its senses and reactions. But don’t worry if you’re still confused! You’re not the only one!

As Rahul Varshney, Co-creator of Foster.fm puts it:

User Experience (UX) and User Interface (UI) are some of the most confused and misused terms in our field. A UI without UX is like a painter slapping paint onto canvas without thought; while UX without UI is like the frame of a sculpture with no paper mache on it. A great product experience starts with UX followed by UI. Both are essential for the product’s success.

Flat vs Realism

Flat design and realistic design. Both are completely different and both can be equally effective in term of design. It has been disputed a lot over which one is better, flat or realism? Each style have different purposes and so it is completely subjective.

Realism really comes into play when things need to be as realistic as possible. Take a plane simulator or a surgeons training simulator for example. They need to be as actual and realistic as possible so that the training that is provided is an effective as it can be. This is so that when the person does the real thing they will have some idea of what to expect. If something like a plane simulator was created using flat design it probably wouldn’t work very well at all and would cause a lot of confusion for the trainee. A realistic environment is essential for things like that. The idea of realism (also known as skeuomorphism) came from trying to make replicas of real life items or objects. It came about during web 2.0 when Steve Jobs designed the iPhone. Things such as buttons were designed to look as real as possible by using shadows, highlights and other details in order to make them look three dimensional on a flat surface. Skeuomorphism was created in order to things look familiar so that users would be more comfortable with how to go about using them.

Skeuomorphism was often used within computer programs within their user interfaces in order to recreate a familiar look to real life controls. A popular example of this was within music editing software where they would closely replicate musical instruments and equipment. The buttons, knobs, sliders and other such controls would be almost perfect duplicates of the ones found on physical devices. It even got to the point where things such as screws and hands would be added on to give as realistic look even though they had no purpose within the user interface. One of the first apps on the iPhone was the calculator app. This app was made to look almost identical to our everyday real life calculator. This was so that people would automatically understand what it was and what to do with it because it looked like a calculator, but it was on a flat screen.

Flat design is also known as a form of minimalist design. It is a style that completely removes any stylistic approaches that would give the look of a three dimensional object such as shadows or gradients. It is generally used in order to enhance the actual usability of an application rather than it look. Flat design is often described as simple, colourful, modern and clean. Illustrations are fall simpler and less complex which results in faster loading times. As well as that, using flat style often results in the content being presented in a much simpler and easy to understand manner.

The flat style was inspired from the international Typographic style, which is also known as the swiss style as well as Modernism. Aspects of these art movements from the 1950s and 1960s have been heavily incorporated into flat design that we use today, over fifty-five years later.

During 2006, Microsoft sealed a mp3 music player called Zune which incorporated elements of flat design. It was clean and simple which made it an easy interface to use. In 2010, Microsoft once again used this flat style when they released the Windows phone 7. The interface within the Windows phone 7 incorporated the use of large coloured tiles, flat imagery and sans-serif typography. This simplistic look was then completed with the use of a grid menu which made it easy to make a selection. This use of slat style appeared to be popular and was well liked and so they used it within the release of Windows 8, Microsoft 365 and the Xbox 360 interfaces. It wasn't until 2013 that Apple decided to follow in Microsofts footsteps and moved away from skeuomorphic design and adapted the flat design look with iOS 7.

Why is skeuomorphic design fading away?

Generally skeuomorphic interfaces tend to take up more screen face than those created using the flat style. One point that has been noted is that skeuomorphic interfaces very rarely have numeric input or feedback which many users will rely on to know if the interface has worked correctly or not. Whilst apple was run by Steve Jobs, it was well known for their heavy usage of skeuomorphic design within their interfaces and applications. It wasn’t until after Steve Jobs had died that Apple looked into whether or not it was a good idea to continue with their incorporation of skeuomorphic design with their new products. It was this debate and decision to move onto a more simplified design that may have resulted in the resignation of Scott Forstall who had been described as "the most vocal and high-ranking proponent of the visual design style favoured by Mr. Jobs”.

Moodboard

A mood board is a digital collage or a physical collage that helps define the direction of a website. A mock up, on the other hand, is a more concrete version of a website which is only a few stops aways from the finished design. In a mood board you can decide the actual visual and UI elements of a website. Among the samples you can put in a mood board are;

  • Images
  • Photography
  • Fonts
  • Colours
  • Graphics
  • Patterns
  • Text
  • Maps

Mood boards are fluid, there is nothing set in stone. The whole idea of them is to gather ideas and inspiration before jumping into formal design. It can determine the direction you can take with your website; for example professional or playful,or the tone you might take which could be light or dark.

Benefits

It helps to avoid any misunderstanding that may stem from trying to describe something verbally. The mood board sets out your thoughts clearly and it shows how you interpret your ideas. By pinning down colours, fonts, images and overall tone, adding the design to the wire frames becomes much easier and as a result, it makes the whole process smoother. With the content in hand, the mood board allows you to focus on design. You really don’t want great content stuck in an unattractive site. The goal is to keep your mood board design focused as possible, but we must remember they are not the design. Your prototype is your design. One last thing to remember is that mood boards do not communicate the final content.

Animated Scrolling

Animated scrolling is one of the most useful and smart aspects of interaction within a website. Before it came about we relied on internal hyperlinks that would bring you straight to the part of the page you needed. On one hand this was great because you got where you wanted to go and fast. On the other there is often a loss of content in that you may not understand that part of the website. This is because you have missed parts on the journey down, it is like skipping chapter five in a book before reading the first five. Whenever you miss a chapter you may miss something and therefore may end up lacking knowledge about the later content. Animated scrolling takes the user to the part of the site they require by quickly scrolling through all the parts before it. Best Practices for Speeding Up Your Web Site

Minimize HTTP Requests

80% of the end-user response time is spent on the front-end. Most of this time is tied up in downloading all the components in the page: images, stylesheets, scripts, Flash, etc. Reducing the number of components in turn reduces the number of HTTP requests required to render the page. This is the key to faster pages. One way to reduce the number of components in the page is to simplify the page's design. But is there a way to build pages with richer content while also achieving fast response times? Here are some techniques for reducing the number of HTTP requests, while still supporting rich page designs.

Combined files are a way to reduce the number of HTTP requests by combining all scripts into a single script, and similarly combining all CSS into a single stylesheet. Combining files is more challenging when the scripts and stylesheets vary from page to page, but making this part of your release process improves response times.

CSS Sprites are the preferred method for reducing the number of image requests. Combine your background images into a single image and use the CSS background-image and background-position properties to display the desired image segment. Image maps combine multiple images into a single image. The overall size is about the same, but reducing the number of HTTP requests speeds up the page. Image maps only work if the images are contiguous in the page, such as a navigation bar. Defining the coordinates of image maps can be tedious and error prone. Using image maps for navigation is not accessible too, so it's not recommended.

Put Stylesheets at the Top

Moving stylesheets to the document HEAD makes pages appear to be loading faster. This is because putting stylesheets in the HEAD allows the page to render progressively. Front-end engineers that care about performance want a page to load progressively; that is, we want the browser to display whatever content it has as soon as possible. This is especially important for pages with a lot of content and for users on slower Internet connections. The importance of giving users visual feedback, such as progress indicators, has been well researched and documented. In our case the HTML page is the progress indicator! When the browser loads the page progressively the header, the navigation bar, the logo at the top, etc. all serve as visual feedback for the user who is waiting for the page. This improves the overall user experience. The problem with putting stylesheets near the bottom of the document is that it prohibits progressive rendering in many browsers, including Internet Explorer. These browsers block rendering to avoid having to redraw elements of the page if their styles change. The user is stuck viewing a blank white page.

Put Scripts at the Bottom

The problem caused by scripts is that they block parallel downloads. The HTTP/1.1 specification suggests that browsers download no more than two components in parallel per hostname. If you serve your images from multiple hostnames, you can get more than two downloads to occur in parallel. While a script is downloading, however, the browser won't start any other downloads, even on different hostnames. In some situations it's not easy to move scripts to the bottom. If, for example, the script uses document.write to insert part of the page's content, it can't be moved lower in the page. There might also be scoping issues. In many cases, there are ways to workaround these situations. An alternative suggestion that often comes up is to use deferred scripts. The DEFER attribute indicates that the script does not contain document.write, and is a clue to browsers that they can continue rendering. Unfortunately, Firefox doesn't support the DEFER attribute. In Internet Explorer, the script may be deferred, but not as much as desired. If a script can be deferred, it can also be moved to the bottom of the page. That will make your web pages load faster.

Make JavaScript and CSS External

Using external files in the real world generally produces faster pages because the JavaScript and CSS files are cached by the browser. JavaScript and CSS that are inlined in HTML documents get downloaded every time the HTML document is requested. This reduces the number of HTTP requests that are needed, but increases the size of the HTML document. On the other hand, if the JavaScript and CSS are in external files cached by the browser, the size of the HTML document is reduced without increasing the number of HTTP requests.
